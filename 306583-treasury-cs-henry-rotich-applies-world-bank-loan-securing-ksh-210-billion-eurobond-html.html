Treasury CS Rotich applies for World Bank loan days after securing KSh 210 billion Eurobond

- The exchequer is seeking for an additional KSh 75 billion loan from World Bank to plug budget deficit

- If approved, the new credit facility will go towards financing some of the projects under the Big Four agenda

- The Treasury had days earlier secured a third round of Eurobond priced at KSh 210 billion to be used in settling old debts, among other things

Barely a month after Kenya secured KSh 210 billion Eurobond, the National Treasury has returned to the foreign lenders to seek for more loans as the government struggles to raise funds for major development projects.

Treasury boss Henry Rotich has reportedly knocked on the door of World Bank looking for what was described as an urgent loan needed to finance President Uhuru Kenyatta's Big Four agenda.

A report by the Daily Nation on Tuesday, May 28, revealed the exchequer had applied for a KSh 75 billion loan in the name of Inclusive Growth and Fiscal Management Development Policy Financing.

The World Bank, the paper reported, was expected to make a decision soon on whether or not to approve Kenya's request for quick credit facility.

Notably, the World Bank had on April 30, 2019, approved some KSh 25.62 billion (US$250 million) International Bank for Reconstruction and Development (IBRD) loan to support Uhuru's affordable housing project which is one of the pillars of the Big Four agenda.

"The Kenya Affordable Housing Finance Project (KAHFP) will support the establishment and operationalization of the Kenya Mortgage Refinance Corporation (KMRC), a largely private sector-owned and non-deposit taking financial institution under the supervision of the Central Bank of Kenya," said Felipe Jaramillo, World Bank Kenya country director.

A few days after securing the KSh 25 billion for the affordable housing finance project which Uhuru officially launched on May 22, the third round of Eurobond priced at KSh 210 billion arrived.

The new debt instrument was successfully sold in London, United Kingdom (UK), on May 15, and will be issued in two tranches.

"The government of Kenya, acting through the National Treasury, successfully priced a new US$2.1 billion (KSh 210 billion), dual-tranche Eurobond of seven-year and 12-year tenors on May 15, 2019, in London, United Kingdom," Rotich said in a statement to newsrooms.

He explained the two Eurobond tranches were priced at 7% interest rate for a seven-year maturity period and 8% interest for a 12-year tenure.

The exchequer stated part of the funds raised will go towards settling old debts including a 2014 Eurobond of KSh 75 billion expected to mature in June 2019, among other debt obligations.

"It will also be used to refinance part or all of the obligations outstanding under the U$750 million (2014 Eurobond) due on June 24, 2019, and potentially part of the other debt obligations," he said.

The loans are being taken despite rising concerns over ballooning public debt which staggered at KSh 5.4 trillion as of May 2019.

Since the Jubilee government came to power in 2013, the public debt has risen from about KSh 1.8 trillion to KSh 5.4 trillion in May 2019 according to the Central Bank of Kenya (CBK) data.

As of September 2018, the total national debt (external and domestic loans) stood at KSh 5.1 trillion, an increase from KSh 4.4 trillion in June 2017.

The debt stood at KSh 3.6 trillion in 2016, KSh 2.8 trillion in 2015, and KSh 2.3 trillion in 2014 according to the Treasury.

Going by the exchequer's projections, the national debt is likely to cross the KSh 7 trillion mark by June 2022 when Uhuru's 10-year reign ends.
